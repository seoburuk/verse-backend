# 1 Corinthians

First Corinthians is Paul's letter to a church troubled by division, immorality, and confused teaching. Corinth was a bustling port city saturated with commerce and pleasure, and that culture had seeped into the church, producing factions, sexual sin, disputes over food offered to idols, abuse of the Lord's Supper, and confusion about spiritual gifts. Paul answers all of this with firmness, yet with the heart of a pastor.

What marks this letter is that even while addressing very concrete, practical problems, Paul keeps returning every answer to the cross and to Christ. The divisions, the boasting in wisdom, the misuse of spiritual gifts — all of it traces back to forgetting that the message of the cross is the power of God. Paul opens by declaring that he determined to know nothing among them except Jesus Christ and him crucified, refusing to rely on human wisdom or eloquence.

## Core Message

First Corinthians repeatedly shows that genuine faith is never proven by spiritual gifts, knowledge, or religious zeal. The declaration in chapter 13 — that speaking in tongues, prophesying, and possessing all knowledge amount to nothing without love — nails down the truth that enthusiastic religious display can never substitute for true godliness. What marks authentic faith is holding firmly to the essential gospel of Christ's cross and resurrection, and living out the love and holiness that gospel produces.

In chapter 15, Paul states plainly that if Christ has not been raised, our faith is in vain and we are still in our sins. This makes clear that Christianity is not a set of moral teachings or life wisdom but a faith grounded in an actual historical event — the resurrection. No amount of cheap comfort or vague religious feeling can reach this kind of certainty. Paul's relentless labor for the gospel and his discipline of his own body flowed directly from his conviction that the sure hope of the resurrection was not in vain.

## Verses Worth Memorizing

> There hath no temptation taken you but such as is common to man: but God is faithful, who will not suffer you to be tempted above that ye are able; but will with the temptation also make a way to escape, that ye may be able to bear it. — 1 Corinthians 10:13

This promise does not mean temptation disappears, but assures believers that God, being faithful, always provides the strength to endure and a way of escape.

> Charity suffereth long, and is kind; charity envieth not... Charity never faileth. — 1 Corinthians 13:4-7, 13

This definition of love describes an act of the will rather than a feeling, a love that does not originate in human nature but flows from Christ.

> For I delivered unto you first of all that which I also received, how that Christ died for our sins according to the scriptures; And that he was buried, and that he rose again the third day according to the scriptures — 1 Corinthians 15:3-4

The heart of the gospel is the historical fact of Christ's death and resurrection, and that it happened according to the Scriptures is the sure foundation of faith.

> Therefore, my beloved brethren, be ye stedfast, unmoveable, always abounding in the work of the Lord, forasmuch as ye know that your labour is not in vain in the Lord. — 1 Corinthians 15:58

Because the resurrection is certain, labor for the Lord is never wasted — a genuine encouragement to the weary believer.

## Why It's Worth Memorizing

The verses of 1 Corinthians continually remind us that the essence of true faith is not impressive gifts or human wisdom but the cross, the resurrection, and love. Memorizing them plants a firm root of faith in an unstable world — holding on to God's faithfulness amid temptation, engraving the true definition of love on the heart, and laboring on the sure hope of the resurrection.
