# Deuteronomy

Deuteronomy is Moses' final sermon, delivered on the plains of Moab just before Israel crosses the Jordan into the promised land. The name means "second law," but this is no mere repetition. The generation that left Egypt has died in the wilderness, and a new generation now stands at the border. Moses retraces forty years of history so this new people will remember who God is and how they are called to live before him.

The backbone of the book is covenant. Moses renews at Moab the covenant made at Sinai, pronouncing blessing for obedience and curse for rebellion in the most solemn terms. Yet this is no transaction in which obedience purchases God's favor. Deuteronomy 7 makes clear that God did not choose Israel because they were great or righteous, but because of his own love and the oath he swore to their fathers. Grace comes first; obedience is the response to grace already given.

## The Central Message

Running through the whole book is a call to undivided devotion to the one true God. The Shema in 6:4-5 is the heart of Deuteronomy and the seedbed of Israel's confession of faith: "Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God is one LORD: and thou shalt love the LORD thy God with all thine heart, and with all thy soul, and with all thy might." This is more than moral compliance. It demands love that claims the whole person, a loyalty that cannot be shared. Deuteronomy has no room for divided worship or for religion reduced to outward ritual.

At the same time, Deuteronomy does not hide the native stubbornness of the human heart. Chapter 30 looks forward to a circumcision of the heart, a day when God himself will change Israel from the inside. True obedience is never merely coerced from without; it flows from a heart made new. This anticipates the new covenant that Jeremiah and Ezekiel will later describe, and it is finally accomplished in Christ. Deuteronomy never settles for cheap religious form. The choice it sets before Israel, between life and death, blessing and curse, is always weighty and real.

## Recommended Verses to Memorize

> Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God is one LORD: And thou shalt love the LORD thy God with all thine heart, and with all thy soul, and with all thy might. And these words, which I command thee this day, shall be in thine heart: And thou shalt teach them diligently unto thy children, and shalt talk of them when thou sittest in thine house, and when thou walkest by the way, and when thou liest down, and when thou risest up.
> Deuteronomy 6:4-9

This is the constitutional text of biblical faith, the confession of one God and the command to love him with the whole person in every moment of life. Memorizing it makes it a daily anchor for worship.

> And thou shalt love the LORD thy God with all thine heart, and with all thy soul, and with all thy might.
> Deuteronomy 6:5

Short but total in its claim, this verse leaves no room for divided devotion. Carrying it in memory turns it into a mirror that examines the shape of our own love for God.

> And he humbled thee, and suffered thee to hunger, and fed thee with manna... that he might make thee know that man doth not live by bread only, but by every word that proceedeth out of the mouth of the LORD doth man live.
> Deuteronomy 8:3

Jesus himself quoted this verse when tempted in the wilderness. It reminds us that dependence on God's word outweighs even our physical needs, and it gives strength to stand in the hour of temptation.

> And now, Israel, what doth the LORD thy God require of thee, but to fear the LORD thy God, to walk in all his ways, and to love him, and to serve the LORD thy God with all thy heart and with all thy soul,
> Deuteronomy 10:12

This verse cuts through legalism to the heart of true religion: reverent fear, love, and wholehearted service, not mere rule-keeping.

> I have set before thee this day life and good, and death and evil... therefore choose life, that both thou and thy seed may live: That thou mayest love the LORD thy God, and that thou mayest obey his voice, and that thou mayest cleave unto him: for he is thy life, and the length of thy days.
> Deuteronomy 30:19-20

This declares both the gravity of choice and the astonishing truth that God himself is our life. Recalling it at moments of decision clarifies what actually matters.

> Be strong and of a good courage, fear not, nor be afraid of them: for the LORD thy God, he it is that doth go with thee; he will not fail thee, nor forsake thee.
> Deuteronomy 31:6

Spoken to Israel before the conquest of Canaan, this promise of God's unfailing presence steadies the heart in every fearful season of life still today.

## Why It Is Good to Memorize This Book

Deuteronomy teaches that faith is never mere emotion or custom, but wholehearted devotion of heart, soul, and strength. Memorizing these verses is not simply accumulating knowledge; it is training the heart to return daily to love for God and to cling to his faithfulness in every unsteady moment. The Shema in particular has been carried on the lips of believers for thousands of years, and each time we recite it we are reminded that worship must claim the whole of us, not merely a part.
