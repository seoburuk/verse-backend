# Ephesians

Ephesians, written by Paul from a Roman prison, does not address a specific crisis so much as lay out in majestic terms God's plan of salvation and the nature of the church. The first three chapters are filled with doctrine, praising God for choosing and predestining us in Christ before the foundation of the world, while the last three chapters describe a life fitting for that grace.

The very structure of the letter mirrors the order of true faith. Only after knowing what God has done and falling before Him in worship does a life that responds to that grace become possible. Though Paul calls himself a prisoner of Jesus Christ, the entire letter is filled not with despair but with awe at the greatness of God's plan.

## Core Message

Ephesians declares as clearly as any book in Scripture that salvation is entirely by God's grace. "For by grace are ye saved through faith; and that not of yourselves: it is the gift of God: Not of works, lest any man should boast" leaves no room anywhere in the process of salvation for human merit. This truth humbles the human ego while directing all glory to God alone.

At the same time, Paul writes that we are his workmanship, created in Christ Jesus unto good works, which God prepared beforehand that we should walk in them. Free grace never justifies a lazy life; rather, it recreates us to actually walk in the good works God had already prepared. The exhortation to put on the whole armor of God later in the letter likewise teaches that the Christian life is spiritual warfare, and that we must stand not in our own strength but in the power of the Lord's might.

## Verses Worth Memorizing

> For by grace are ye saved through faith; and that not of yourselves: it is the gift of God: Not of works, lest any man should boast. — Ephesians 2:8-9

This declaration that salvation is entirely a gift excludes every form of human boasting at its root.

> For we are his workmanship, created in Christ Jesus unto good works, which God hath before ordained that we should walk in them. — Ephesians 2:10

This verse shows that free grace and a good life are not opposed to one another — grace is itself the source of good works.

> And be ye kind one to another, tenderhearted, forgiving one another, even as God for Christ's sake hath forgiven you. — Ephesians 4:32

Our forgiveness of others is not self-generated; it must reflect the forgiveness of Christ that we ourselves first received.

> Finally, my brethren, be strong in the Lord, and in the power of his might. Put on the whole armour of God, that ye may be able to stand against the wiles of the devil. — Ephesians 6:10-18

In spiritual battle our strength is never our own but the Lord's power alone, and we must put on the armor to draw on that power.

## Why It's Worth Memorizing

The verses of Ephesians repeatedly drive home that salvation is entirely of grace, tearing down self-righteousness, while also holding together a whole life shaped by that grace — good works, mutual forgiveness, and steadiness in spiritual warfare. Engraving this union of free grace and fitting obedience on the heart does much to keep faith in balance.
