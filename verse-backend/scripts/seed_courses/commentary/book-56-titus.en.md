# Titus — Grace That Produces Good Works

Titus is a pastoral letter Paul wrote to his co-worker Titus, whom he had left behind on the island of Crete. Crete was notorious for moral disorder, and Paul speaks bluntly of its culture as he charges Titus to set the church in order — appointing elders in every city, silencing the empty talk of false teachers, and instructing believers of every generation and station in how they ought to live. These are the practical concerns of the letter.

Yet the true strength of this short epistle lies in the theological statements woven between its practical commands. Paul compresses into a few sentences how grace has appeared and what it teaches, making clear that salvation begun by grace and not by works must nevertheless lead to a life zealous for good works. Titus is one of the clearest letters in Scripture for untangling the relationship between grace and works.

## The Central Message

The order Titus teaches is unmistakable. Salvation came not by works of righteousness which we have done, but according to God's mercy. This is precisely where grace differs from cheap grace. Grace is given freely, but it never ends in a fruitless life. The declaration that grace has appeared, bringing salvation to all men, teaching us to deny ungodliness and worldly lusts and to live soberly, righteously, and godly, shows that grace is not a license for indulgence but a power that leads to holiness.

Paul also says that Christ gave himself for us, that he might redeem us and purify unto himself a peculiar people, zealous of good works. The very purpose of Christ's redemptive work is to form a people who bear the fruit of good deeds. Doctrine and ethics, theology and life, can never be separated — this is the consistent testimony of Titus.

## Verses Worth Memorizing

> For the grace of God that bringeth salvation hath appeared to all men, Teaching us that, denying ungodliness and worldly lusts, we should live soberly, righteously, and godly, in this present world; Looking for that blessed hope, and the glorious appearing of the great God and our Saviour Jesus Christ; — Titus 2:11-14

Grace is not an escape but a teacher. True grace always instructs and trains believers toward a godly life.

> Not by works of righteousness which we have done, but according to his mercy he saved us, by the washing of regeneration, and renewing of the Holy Ghost; — Titus 3:5

Few verses state more plainly that the starting point of salvation is not human merit but the sheer mercy of God. There is nothing to boast in — only gratitude is a fitting response.

## Why It's Worth Memorizing

The verses of Titus restore the proper order between grace and works. They remind believers that salvation is by grace alone, yet those who truly receive that grace will always move toward a life zealous for good works.
