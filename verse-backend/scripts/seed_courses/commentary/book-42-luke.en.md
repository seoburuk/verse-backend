# Luke

Luke, the physician, dedicated his gospel to a man named Theophilus, writing "in order" with careful historical precision. It forms the first of a two-volume work continued in the book of Acts. Writing for a Gentile, Greek-speaking audience, Luke traces the life of Jesus with unusual historical detail, including the birth narrative, childhood accounts, and several parables found nowhere else in Scripture.

The most striking feature of Luke's gospel is Jesus' attention to the overlooked and outcast. Shepherds, women, tax collectors, sinners, and Gentiles appear throughout as objects of grace. Luke portrays Jesus as one who came to seek and save all humanity, especially those the world had written off, insisting from beginning to end that salvation is not the property of any single class or nation but God's plan for all people.

## The Central Message

The theological center of Luke is Jesus who came "to seek and to save that which was lost" (19:10). This is not mere sentimental compassion; it rests on the biblical understanding that human beings cannot find their own way back to God, that they are, in fact, lost. Salvation happens only because God moves first. The three parables of chapter 15, especially the prodigal son, capture this truth with unmatched beauty. The son who squandered everything and left his father does not devise a plan to earn his way back; while he is still "a great way off," the father sees him and runs to embrace him. Grace always begins with God's initiative.

At the same time, Luke never treats repentance lightly. The prodigal "arose, and came to his father," genuinely confessing his condition rather than offering hollow regret. And on the cross, Jesus prays, "Father, forgive them; for they know not what they do" (23:34), the high point of grace in this gospel. That Christ intercedes for forgiveness even in the act of his own substitutionary death declares, from start to finish, that salvation rests not on human merit but on the sovereign mercy of God.

## Recommended Verses to Memorize

> And the angel said unto them, Fear not: for, behold, I bring you good tidings of great joy, which shall be to all people. For unto you is born this day in the city of David a Saviour, which is Christ the Lord.
> Luke 2:10-11

This announcement of the Savior's birth is the heart of the Christmas story. Memorizing it renews, every time, the sense that Christ's coming is not merely a historical event but genuine good news for all people.

> For this my son was dead, and is alive again; he was lost, and is found. And they began to be merry.
> Luke 15:24 (the parable of the prodigal son)

The whole parable of the prodigal son paints the gospel of grace in a single picture. Memorizing this verse plants the assurance that no matter how far we have wandered, the Father runs to meet us when we return.

> For the Son of man is come to seek and to save that which was lost.
> Luke 19:10

This verse compresses the whole purpose of Jesus' mission into one sentence. For those of us prone to self-righteousness, it is a constant reminder that we were all once lost, and are saved only because Christ came seeking us.

> Then said Jesus, Father, forgive them; for they know not what they do.
> Luke 23:34

This prayer, offered from the cross for the very people crucifying him, reveals the depth of Christ's love. Memorizing it keeps us from forgetting the costly sacrifice on which our own forgiveness rests.

## Why It Is Good to Memorize This Book

Luke portrays the heart of God seeking the lost with more warmth and specificity than any other gospel. Memorizing these verses plants deep in the heart the gospel of grace: that we had no standing to come to God ourselves, yet he came seeking us first. The parable of the prodigal son and Christ's prayer of forgiveness from the cross especially carry the power to remind us daily that salvation, unattainable by any human goodness, is given solely through the mercy of Christ.
