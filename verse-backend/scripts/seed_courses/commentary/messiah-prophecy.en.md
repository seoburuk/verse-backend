# Messiah Prophecy

## Course Introduction

This course follows a remarkable thread that binds the whole of Scripture into one: the promise of the Messiah, the Savior, inscribed beforehand in the Old Testament, and the fulfillment of that promise in Jesus Christ in the New Testament. Over the course of centuries, in different eras and circumstances, the prophets of the Old Testament bore witness to the One who was to come. He is the Son of David, born of a virgin, the suffering servant, and the humble King.

These prophecies are not fragmentary predictions but pieces of God's grand and consistent plan of salvation. Isaiah, Micah, Zechariah, and the psalmists did not know one another, yet the picture they painted comes together, upon reaching the New Testament, with astonishing precision in one figure: Jesus. Jesus Himself, after His resurrection, opened to the disciples on the road to Emmaus "the things concerning himself," "beginning at Moses and all the prophets."

To study the Messianic prophecies, then, is to embark on a journey of discovering how the Bible is one story from the hand of one Author, namely God. The Old and New Testaments are not separate books but one gospel joined together as promise and fulfillment.

## The Heart of the Message

The Messianic prophecies are read above all as evidence of **God's faithfulness**. From the moment God promised in Genesis 3:15 a seed who would crush the serpent's head, He never forgot or withdrew that promise. To Abraham, to David, and through the prophets, that promise grew ever clearer, and at last, "when the fulness of the time was come," it was fully accomplished in Christ. The fulfillment of prophecy proves that God is One who surely keeps His word.

All these prophecies also lie within the stream of the **covenant of grace**. The saints of the Old Testament were saved by faith as they looked forward to the coming Christ, and the saints of the New Testament are saved by that same faith as they look back to the Christ who has come. From Abraham to the believer of today, the way of salvation has always been one thing alone: faith in Christ. The covenant is not many but one covenant of grace centered on Christ.

Above all, the Messianic prophecies foreshow the **threefold office** of Christ—prophet, priest, and king. He is the true prophet who declares the will of God, the true priest who offers His own body to atone for the sins of His people, and the true king who rules over all the world. The mystery by which the prophecy of the suffering servant and the prophecy of the king of glory meet in one Person is brought to completion only through the cross and the resurrection.

## The Old Testament Points to Christ

**Genesis**

Genesis opens with humanity's fall, yet at that very scene God already lays the thread of redemption. Genesis 3:15 is the first gospel promise in all of Scripture: the seed of the woman would crush the serpent's head, a sovereign declaration announcing that Satan's power would be broken at the cross. The genealogies that follow — Abraham, Isaac, Judah — are not mere records of lineage but the trajectory of a covenant in which God, through one chosen seed, would bless all nations, a covenant whose final destination is Christ alone.

The appearance of Melchizedek, the priest-king; the lamb provided in place of Isaac; and the prophecy that the scepter would not depart from Judah until Shiloh comes — all move toward fulfillment not by human effort but within God's gracious predestination. From its first pages, Genesis shows that salvation rests not on man's merit but on the Lamb God Himself provides.

**Exodus**

Exodus tells of a people delivered from bondage to sin, but at the center of that deliverance stands blood. Only where the blood of a lamb without blemish was applied to the doorposts did the angel of judgment pass over — establishing from the outset the gospel principle that no human deed or moral improvement can turn away wrath, only substitutionary blood. The Passover lamb was a shadow; the substance is Christ, who died on the cross without a bone being broken.

God's self-revelation as "I AM WHO I AM" directly anticipates Christ's own declaration, "before Abraham was, I am," revealing that the God of Exodus is the very Christ who would become flesh. Likewise the water bursting from the rock, which sustained the thirsty people in the wilderness, prefigures the spiritual Rock who is Christ.

**Leviticus**

Under the form of a sacrificial law code, Leviticus already sketches the theology of the cross. The sin offering, the principle that life is redeemed only through the shedding of an animal's blood, and the scapegoat that bears the people's sin outside the camp on the Day of Atonement — all are mere shadows whose substance is Christ, who shed His own blood outside the gate at Calvary. As Hebrews declares, the blood of bulls and goats could never take away sin; only the once-for-all sacrifice of Christ, the spotless High Priest, accomplished perfect atonement.

The declaration that "the life of the flesh is in the blood" runs through all of Leviticus, insisting absolutely that man cannot save himself and that substitutionary blood is required. This is no cheap grace — it carries the weighty truth that God's justice must be satisfied before a sinner can live.

**Numbers**

Numbers shows both Israel's failure in the wilderness march and God's unshaken faithfulness. When the people, grumbling against God, were bitten by fiery serpents, everyone who looked at the bronze serpent Moses lifted up on a pole lived — an event Christ Himself explicitly applied to His own crucifixion. The principle of justification by faith alone was already proclaimed in the wilderness: dying sinners are saved not by any deed of their own but simply by looking to the Christ who was lifted up.

Balaam's prophecy that "a star shall come out of Jacob" likewise points to the Messiah-King who would surely come at God's appointed time, even though it was not yet fulfilled. Numbers testifies that even when God's people fail, His redemptive plan never collapses.

**Deuteronomy**

Deuteronomy is the book in which Moses, with the promised land in view, renews the covenant for the next generation — and at its heart stands the promise of a coming Prophet. "The LORD your God will raise up for you a prophet like me from among you, from your brothers — it is to him you shall listen" points beyond Moses to the ultimate Mediator, Christ Himself. Jesus' rebuke that if they had believed Moses they would have believed Him testifies that the Old and New Testaments are not two separate stories but one unified history of redemption.

The statute that one hanged on a tree is cursed likewise anticipates, as Galatians makes explicit, that Christ Himself became a curse for us, redeeming us from the curse of the law. Even within the language of judgment, the gospel of grace was already hidden.

**Ruth**

Ruth is a short but profound account of a Moabite woman who turns to the God of Israel and meets Boaz, her kinsman-redeemer. The legal process of the goel — redeeming a dead man's inheritance and taking the widow as wife — prefigures the gospel in which Christ pays the price to reclaim our lost inheritance and takes as His bride those who were once slaves to sin and death. By requiring that the redeemer be a kinsman, Ruth quietly reveals the necessity of the incarnation: Christ had to take on flesh like ours in order to redeem us.

That a Gentile woman is grafted into the Messiah's genealogy testifies that God's salvation comes by grace alone, not by lineage or merit, and marks the starting point of the line that runs through David to Christ.

**1 Samuel**

1 Samuel is the book in which the narrative's center shifts from Saul's failed kingship to David's anointing. Hannah's prophetic song — "He will give strength to His king and exalt the horn of His anointed" — points immediately to the Davidic dynasty, but ultimately to the Christ, the Messiah, who would be anointed by the Spirit and established as the true King. The contrast between Saul and David prepares the truth that any king established by human choice will fail, while the King whom God Himself chooses and anoints will never fail.

This shows that God's rule is founded not on human selection but on His absolute sovereignty, forming the overture to Israel's royal history that leads to Christ, the true King.

**2 Samuel**

At the heart of 2 Samuel lies the Davidic Covenant. God promises David that his seed would sit on the throne forever and would be called the Son of God — a promise not exhausted in Solomon but fully realized in Christ, as the angel declared to Mary: "the Lord God will give to him the throne of his father David, and he will reign over the house of Jacob forever." This promise that David's house would never fall is an unconditional covenant grounded in God's own faithfulness, independent of the rise and fall of human dynasties.

This covenant is the root of the New Testament gospel genealogies, which testify that Christ is both the Son of David and the Son of God.

**2 Kings**

2 Kings is largely a record of the kings' apostasy and judgment, yet near its opening, in a single brief scene, Elijah is taken up into heaven without passing through death — prefiguring Christ's own ascension after His resurrection. Elijah's being carried up in a whirlwind shows in miniature the ultimate pattern of one who, having finished his earthly work, returns in glory to the right hand of God.

This shows that Christ's ascension was not an isolated event but the climax of a hope God had been planting among His people all along.

**1 Chronicles**

1 Chronicles reiterates and reinforces the Davidic Covenant already given in 2 Samuel. Reaffirming to a discouraged, returned exile community that David's seed would hold an everlasting throne serves to proclaim that even when the temple and the dynasty seemed to lie in ruins, God's redemptive plan had never been revoked. Through genealogy after genealogy, the Chronicler traces David's seed as unbroken, leading at last to Christ.

This testifies to God's providential faithfulness — that even amid what appears, to human eyes, as failure and ruin, He surely accomplishes what He has purposed.

**Job**

Job prefigures Christ at the very peak of a confession of faith reached through the depths of suffering. In the midst of unbearable pain, Job declares, "I know that my Redeemer lives, and at the last he will stand upon the earth," proclaiming a resurrection hope that even after his skin is destroyed, he will see God apart from his flesh. This confidence is given not by human reason or circumstance but by God's own revelation, and it ultimately points to Christ, who would rise from the dead and judge both the living and the dead.

Job's confession stands as one of the oldest testimonies to resurrection faith in Scripture, showing that suffering never negates God's faithfulness but instead drives the sufferer toward the hope of resurrection.

**Psalms**

The Psalms are not merely an ancient hymnbook of Israel — they are songs of Christ that the Holy Spirit caused David and the other psalmists to sing beforehand. Even when David sang of his own suffering and kingship, his words reach beyond his own experience to point to the coming Messiah's cross, resurrection, and eternal reign. Psalm 22 in particular records the scene of Calvary as though it had been witnessed firsthand — the piercing of hands and feet, the casting of lots for His garments, the disjointing of His bones, and the very cry, "My God, My God, why hast thou forsaken me?" Psalms 16 and 118 go on to declare that His death was not the end but led to the triumph of resurrection, His body not seeing corruption but rising from the grave. Because the whole of Scripture is the inerrant Word of God, it is no accident that these songs, written centuries before the fact, foretold the exact sufferings and glory of Jesus Christ down to the smallest detail — it is written evidence of God's absolutely sovereign plan.

At the same time, the Psalms are a summons to worship Christ. He is pictured as the Shepherd (Psalm 23), the King of glory to be exalted (Psalms 24, 110), the eternal High Priest after the order of Melchizedek (Psalm 110), and the King who receives dominion to bring the gospel to the nations (Psalm 72). Such a Christ cannot be met with cheap sentiment; He calls us to worship Him with true repentance and reverent fear. The psalmists never hid their own sin and weakness, yet they sang of a hope of salvation and restoration found only in Christ. This is the center every generation of believers must hold fast to when reading the Psalms — the true hero of the Psalms is not David, but David's Son and David's Lord, Jesus Christ.

**Song of Solomon**

On the surface the Song of Solomon is a love song between bridegroom and bride, but at its depths it foreshadows the mysterious covenant of love between Christ and His church. The bridegroom's unwavering love and devotion toward his bride, his calling of her, and her answering love, prefigure the covenantal love with which Christ freely loves His own people and gave Himself for them. This can never be reduced to mere human sentiment or romance; it is fully understood only in the sacrificial love of Christ, who loved His bride the church even unto laying down His life on the cross.

**Isaiah**

Among all the prophetic books of the Old Testament, Isaiah unveils Christ with the widest scope and the greatest depth. From the Immanuel prophecy of chapter 7 to the names given in chapter 9 — Wonderful, Counsellor, The mighty God, The everlasting Father, The Prince of Peace — Isaiah declares that the coming Messiah would not merely be a human leader but God Himself come in the flesh. The promise that a virgin would conceive and bear a son is inseparably joined to the divine titles given to that son, foretelling that Christ's humanity and deity would dwell fully together in one person.

Yet the center and summit of the book is the Servant Song of chapter 53. Here Isaiah describes in painstaking detail that the coming Messiah would not first ascend to honor and glory, but would be despised and rejected, would carry our griefs, would be wounded for our transgressions, and would be led like a lamb to the slaughter, silent before His executioners. He was pierced for our transgressions, He was bruised for our iniquities, the chastisement of our peace was upon Him, and with His stripes we are healed — this declaration previews the whole gospel of substitution in prophetic form. This is no cheap grace; it is the wrath of God poured out in full upon the Son in the sinner's place, and the reconciliation purchased at that cost.

Isaiah does not stop there. He also foretells the Messiah's resurrection and the triumph that follows, the extension of salvation to the nations, the outpouring of the Spirit, and the mission to proclaim the acceptable year of new-covenant grace. The voice crying in the wilderness in chapter 40, the gentle Servant who will not break a bruised reed in chapter 42, and the proclamation of good tidings to the poor in chapter 61 — this is the very passage Christ Himself read in the synagogue at Nazareth and declared fulfilled. In this way, Isaiah paints, in one unified picture drawn centuries in advance, the whole course of Christ's life: His birth and ministry, His suffering, death, and resurrection, and the consummation of salvation for the whole world.

**Jeremiah**

Jeremiah wept over a collapsing nation, yet even amid the ruins he looked ahead to the true King who would be raised up. He declared that a "righteous Branch" would spring from David's line to reign wisely and execute justice and righteousness in the earth — a testimony that God had never abandoned His covenant with David, even when the dynasty seemed to be cut off. That King would be no mere man, but One called "THE LORD OUR RIGHTEOUSNESS" — God Himself.

Jeremiah also foretold the strange conception of a child born of a virgin, and a new covenant to replace the old. Unlike the covenant given at Sinai and written on tablets of stone, this new covenant would be written on the hearts of men — sealed by the blood Christ shed on the cross.

**Ezekiel**

Ministering among the exiles in Babylon, Ezekiel sternly rebuked Israel's false shepherds and announced that God Himself would raise up one Shepherd, a Branch of David, to feed His flock. In the very days when David's dynasty seemed erased from history, this was a promise that the royal seed would never be cut off, but would culminate in Christ, the true Shepherd.

**Daniel**

Through the dreams and visions of foreign kings, Daniel unveiled the ultimate triumph of God's kingdom. He saw "one like the Son of man" coming with the clouds of heaven to the Ancient of Days, receiving an everlasting dominion and kingdom — a vision Jesus fulfilled when He called Himself "the Son of man." Daniel's famous prophecy of the seventy weeks also revealed, with striking precision, the timing of the Messiah's coming to finish transgression, His being cut off and killed, and the ending of the sacrificial system through His atoning sacrifice.

**Hosea · Joel · Micah · Haggai**

These four minor prophets served in different times and circumstances, yet spoke with one voice concerning the coming Messiah. Hosea sang of the One who would ultimately conquer even the power of death and the grave. Joel proclaimed the promise of salvation open to all who call upon His name. Micah pointed precisely to a small, unremarkable town, Bethlehem, revealing that the One born there had, in fact, existed from of old, from everlasting. Haggai told the people rebuilding the temple that its latter glory would exceed the former — a promise fulfilled when Christ Himself came to that very temple.

**Zechariah**

Among the twelve minor prophets, Zechariah is unusually rich in messianic prophecy, spanning both the Messiah's first and second comings, His humiliation and His exaltation. He portrayed with precision the King entering Jerusalem in humility, riding on the colt of a donkey — not a warhorse of conquest, but the mount of a King bringing peace and salvation. Yet that humble King would be rejected by His own people. Zechariah foretold in detail His betrayal for thirty pieces of silver, the money cast into the temple, and finally His body being pierced. The shepherd struck so that the sheep are scattered points to the disciples scattering at the cross; the mourning over the One who was pierced points to Israel looking on Him, both at the cross and in the coming day of His return. In this single book, Zechariah densely weaves together the Priest-King, the chief cornerstone, and the One who is truly God and truly man.

**Malachi**

The last prophet of the Old Testament, Malachi rebuked Israel's formalism and coldness of heart while foretelling a messenger who would prepare the way for the coming Messiah. He described the Lord suddenly coming to His own temple, together with a forerunner who would turn the hearts of the people in the spirit and power of Elijah — precisely fulfilled when John the Baptist cried out in the wilderness, preparing the way for Christ. That the last book of the Old Testament ends by pointing to the first event of the New Testament, the appearing of John the Baptist, shows once again that the whole of Scripture is one unbroken story.

## Recommended Verses to Memorize

> Therefore the Lord himself shall give you a sign; Behold, a virgin shall conceive, and bear a son, and shall call his name Immanuel.

**Isaiah 7:14**
A verse prophesying the virgin birth and "Immanuel (God with us)," fulfilled in the birth of Jesus in Matthew 1. It engraves in the heart the core of the gospel: that God Himself came among us.

> For unto us a child is born, unto us a son is given: and the government shall be upon his shoulder: and his name shall be called Wonderful, Counsellor, The mighty God, The everlasting Father, The Prince of Peace.

**Isaiah 9:6**
A verse proclaiming that the coming Messiah is the mighty God Himself. It lets us hold that the One who came as a child is at the same time the King of all the universe, and so recall the true meaning of the Nativity.

> But he was wounded for our transgressions, he was bruised for our iniquities: the chastisement of our peace was upon him; and with his stripes we are healed.

**Isaiah 53:5**
A verse like the summit of the Old Testament gospel, portraying the atonement of the suffering servant. It engraves deep that the cross of Jesus was for my sin, setting the weight of grace upon the heart.

> All we like sheep have gone astray; we have turned every one to his own way; and the LORD hath laid on him the iniquity of us all.

**Isaiah 53:6**
A verse proclaiming that the sin of all humanity was laid upon one Person. The truth that I too was a lost sheep, yet He bore my sin, stirs a heart of humility and gratitude.

> But thou, Bethlehem Ephratah, though thou be little among the thousands of Judah, yet out of thee shall he come forth unto me that is to be ruler in Israel; whose goings forth have been from of old, from everlasting.

**Micah 5:2**
A verse precisely prophesying that the Messiah would be born in Bethlehem, fulfilled in Matthew 2. It shows how detailed and certain God's plan is, and so strengthens our trust.

> My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?

**Psalm 22:1**
A verse in which David's lament is fulfilled in the cry of Jesus upon the cross. It lets us realize that Jesus bore even the agony of being forsaken by God for our sake.

> For dogs have compassed me: the assembly of the wicked have inclosed me: they pierced my hands and my feet.

**Psalm 22:16**
An astonishing prophecy describing the manner of crucifixion hundreds of years before it was practiced. Before the precision of prophecy and fulfillment, it reaffirms the divine origin of Scripture.

> They part my garments among them, and cast lots upon my vesture.

**Psalm 22:18**
A verse foreshadowing the event of the soldiers casting lots to divide Jesus' garments, fulfilled just so in John 19. The fulfillment of detailed prophecy testifies to God's sovereign providence.

> Rejoice greatly, O daughter of Zion; shout, O daughter of Jerusalem: behold, thy King cometh unto thee: he is just, and having salvation; lowly, and riding upon an ass, and upon a colt the foal of an ass.

**Zechariah 9:9**
A verse prophesying the Messiah entering Jerusalem as a humble King riding on a donkey, fulfilled in Jesus' entry on Palm Sunday. It teaches that His kingship is not the power of the world but the kingship of humility and peace.

> The LORD said unto my Lord, Sit thou at my right hand, until I make thine enemies thy footstool.

**Psalm 110:1**
A verse prophesying that the Messiah is the King seated at God's right hand, the most frequently quoted verse in the New Testament. It sets our eyes on the present reign of the risen and ascended Christ, and so strengthens our hope.

## Why It Is Good to Memorize This Course

Memorizing the Messianic prophecy verses is a work of confirming deep in the heart that the Old and New Testaments are joined as one gospel. Leaping across centuries and many authors to converge upon one figure, Jesus Christ, these prophecies powerfully testify that the Bible came not from human imagination but from the plan of God. When you hold these verses in your heart, the assurance of faith stands firm, and before any doubt you can boldly confess that Jesus is the very Messiah for whom the Old Testament waited so long. The one who knows the God who kept His promise does not waver even before the promises not yet fulfilled.
