# Genesis

## Introduction to Genesis

Genesis is the book that opens the entire Bible. Its Hebrew title, "Bereshith," comes from its first word, "In the beginning," and the name we know, Genesis, simply means "the book of origins." Traditionally attributed to Moses, it majestically unfolds how God made the world and humanity, how sin entered creation, and how God began His work of redemption through one man and one family.

Genesis divides naturally into two parts. Chapters 1 through 11 tell the story of all humanity—creation, the fall, the flood, and the tower of Babel—while chapters 12 through 50 trace the story of one covenant people through Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph. This movement is no accident. In the very place where the whole world had collapsed under sin, God calls one man, Abraham, and gives him the promise that "all families of the earth shall be blessed."

Genesis, then, is not merely a tale from long ago but a root-book that tells us who God is and who we are. The seeds planted here blossom fully across the whole of Scripture and come to their flower in Jesus Christ.

## The Heart of the Message

Reading Genesis, the eye is drawn first to the **absolute sovereignty of God**. The world did not arise on its own but was made solely by God's word. The declaration "Let there be light: and there was light" shows that creation is the fruit of God's free and sovereign will. Humanity, too, is no product of chance but a dignified being made "in the image of God." This truth is the ground of every person's dignity and of the very purpose of life.

At the same time, Genesis honestly bears witness to humanity's **fall**. Through Adam's disobedience, sin and death entered the world, and man was left unable to save himself. Yet even in the seat of judgment, God does not withdraw His grace. Genesis 3:15, the promise of the woman's seed who would crush the serpent's head, is called the first gospel (the protoevangelium) and points forward to the coming Christ.

Above all, Genesis is a book of **covenant**. God makes a covenant with Abraham and keeps it not by human merit but by grace alone. "And he believed in the LORD; and he counted it to him for righteousness" (15:6) shows that the gospel of justification by faith is rooted in Genesis long before the New Testament. God's salvation was grace from beginning to end.

## Recommended Verses to Memorize

> In the beginning God created the heaven and the earth.

**Genesis 1:1**
The first sentence that grounds the whole Bible. To hold this single verse in your heart is to receive a firm anchor for every moment of life: that the world and your own existence began with God.

> So God created man in his own image, in the image of God created he him; male and female created he them.

**Genesis 1:27**
A verse that holds the source of human dignity and identity. Remembering that you were made in the image of God nurtures a refusal to treat yourself or your neighbor carelessly.

> And I will make of thee a great nation, and I will bless thee, and make thy name great; and thou shalt be a blessing.

**Genesis 12:2**
This is the calling of Abraham and the starting point of God's plan of salvation for the whole world. It sets in your heart the grand design by which God blesses all nations through one man.

> And he believed in the LORD; and he counted it to him for righteousness.

**Genesis 15:6**
A jewel of a verse in the Old Testament that compresses the gospel of justification by faith. It presses deep into us that our righteousness is not earned by works but received from God as a gift through faith.

> But as for you, ye thought evil against me; but God meant it unto good, to bring to pass, as it is this day, to save much people alive.

**Genesis 50:20**
Through Joseph's confession, this verse teaches the providence of God. The confidence that God works good even in moments of suffering and betrayal becomes a great comfort when we pass through the dark valleys of life.

## Why It Is Good to Memorize This Book

Memorizing the key verses of Genesis is a work of strengthening the very roots of faith. The origin of the world, the dignity of humanity, the reality of sin, and the promise of a covenant begun in grace are all compressed into these short verses. When you hold these words in your heart, you can always recall the story of God's salvation that runs through the whole Bible, and you gain an unshakable standard before every decision, great and small. The one who knows the beginning can walk boldly toward the end.
