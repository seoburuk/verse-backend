# Matthew

Matthew, the former tax collector also called Levi, wrote his gospel with Jewish readers in view, and it opens the New Testament canon. Beginning with a genealogy that traces Jesus back through David to Abraham, the book builds a sustained case that Jesus is the Messiah long promised in the Hebrew Scriptures. The recurring phrase "that it might be fulfilled" is no accident; it is the spine of Matthew's argument.

The gospel is structured around five great discourses of Jesus (the Sermon on the Mount, the mission discourse, the parables, teaching on the church, and the Olivet discourse), echoing the five books of Moses and presenting Jesus as a new and greater Moses, the true King, and the one who brings God's kingdom to fulfillment. Matthew is not merely a biography; it is a carefully built theological argument about who Jesus is and what it costs to follow him.

## The Central Message

At the center of Matthew stands the kingdom of heaven. Jesus begins his ministry calling people to repent (4:17), and the Sermon on the Mount overturns the world's assumptions about what that kingdom looks like. Blessed are the poor in spirit, those who mourn, the meek — this is a direct assault on worldly measures of success. The kingdom cannot be earned by human effort or credentials; it comes entirely by grace, and yet it demands of its citizens a righteousness surpassing that of the religious elite of the day (5:20), a standard that mere outward observance can never reach.

Matthew refuses cheap religion at every turn. "Not every one that saith unto me, Lord, Lord, shall enter into the kingdom of heaven; but he that doeth the will of my Father which is in heaven" (7:21) makes plain that lip service is not enough. At the same time, from the very first chapter Matthew announces that Jesus came to save his people from their sins (1:21), and the whole narrative moves toward the cross and resurrection where that redeeming grace is finally accomplished. The King who reigns is the same King who dies on a cross to open the door of his kingdom.

## Recommended Verses to Memorize

> Blessed are the poor in spirit: for theirs is the kingdom of heaven... Blessed are they which are persecuted for righteousness' sake: for theirs is the kingdom of heaven.
> Matthew 5:3-10

The Beatitudes overturn every worldly measure of success and happiness. Memorizing them keeps before us what God, not the world, calls blessed.

> But seek ye first the kingdom of God, and his righteousness; and all these things shall be added unto you.
> Matthew 6:33

When priorities blur, this verse reorders life around what matters most, promising that God provides for those who seek his kingdom first.

> Ask, and it shall be given you; seek, and ye shall find; knock, and it shall be opened unto you:
> Matthew 7:7

Even when prayer feels difficult, this short imperative reopens the door back to God.

> Come unto me, all ye that labour and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest. Take my yoke upon you, and learn of me; for I am meek and lowly in heart: and ye shall find rest unto your souls: For my yoke is easy, and my burden is light.
> Matthew 11:28-30

An invitation to those crushed under the weight of trying to earn their own righteousness. This verse reminds us again and again that true rest is found in Christ, not in religious achievement.

> Thou shalt love the Lord thy God with all thy heart, and with all thy soul, and with all thy mind. This is the first and great commandment. And the second is like unto it, Thou shalt love thy neighbour as thyself.
> Matthew 22:37-39

The summary of the whole law, this verse anchors faith not in rule-keeping but in love toward God and neighbor.

> Go ye therefore, and teach all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Ghost: Teaching them to observe all things whatsoever I have commanded you: and, lo, I am with you alway, even unto the end of the world.
> Matthew 28:19-20

The risen King's final command and promise. When the mission feels beyond our strength, this assurance of his abiding presence supplies both comfort and courage.

## Why It Is Good to Memorize This Book

Memorizing Matthew's verses writes into the heart what kind of King Jesus is and what kind of kingdom he brings. From the Beatitudes that invert the world's standards, to the invitation into a rest that self-righteousness can never earn, to the final command sent to the whole world, these words pull us out of complacent, cheap religion and into real obedience and trust.
