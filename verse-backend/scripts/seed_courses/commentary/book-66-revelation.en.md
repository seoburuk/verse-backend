# Revelation

Revelation is the record of the revelation of Jesus Christ given to the apostle John while exiled on the island of Patmos — the final book of the New Testament and the conclusion of the entire Bible. Written to seven churches in Asia Minor under persecution, it unveils, in the language of symbol and vision, the reign of Christ the Lamb, a rule far greater than the visible power of the Roman Empire.

The book moves through letters to the seven churches, a vision of the heavenly throne and the Lamb opening the seals, the judgments of the seven trumpets and seven bowls, and finally the last judgment and the new heaven and new earth. Amid its difficult symbolism, one message remains constant: the sovereign of history is neither Caesar nor any earthly power, but the Lamb who was slain and rose again.

## Core Message

Revelation reminds a suffering church of God's absolute sovereignty. The judgments of the seals, trumpets, and bowls are not random catastrophes but ordered judgments proceeding from the hand of him who sits on the throne and the Lamb, showing that every event in history unfolds under his rule. Whatever the visible circumstances, this gives assurance that ultimate victory already belongs to Christ.

At the same time, the book never softens the wrath of God awaiting those who refuse to repent. Its scenes of judgment repeatedly show God as a holy judge who deals seriously with sin, and coldly record human hardness of heart that refuses to repent even amid catastrophe. True hope lies not in escaping calamity but in the new heaven and new earth promised to those washed by the blood of the Lamb and clothed in white. From beginning to end, the book is centered on Christ — every vision and symbol converges on a single point: the slain Lamb alone is worthy of worship.

## Verses Worth Memorizing

> I am Alpha and Omega, the beginning and the ending, saith the Lord, which is, and which was, and which is to come, the Almighty. (Revelation 1:8)

This declares the Almighty Lord who personally holds the beginning and end of history, confirming that no circumstance falls outside his sovereignty.

> Behold, I stand at the door, and knock: if any man hear my voice, and open the door, I will come in to him, and will sup with him, and he with me. (Revelation 3:20)

Spoken to the lukewarm church of Laodicea, this is an invitation to genuine repentance and personal fellowship, addressed to those settled into formal, empty religion.

> And God shall wipe away all tears from their eyes; and there shall be no more death, neither sorrow, nor crying, neither shall there be any more pain: for the former things are passed away. (Revelation 21:4)

This shows the climax of comfort to be completed in the new heaven and new earth, giving every suffering saint grounds for ultimate hope.

> I am Alpha and Omega, the beginning and the end, the first and the last. (Revelation 22:13)

Closing out the entire Bible, this declaration once more fixes Christ as the beginning and end of all things.

> Even so, come, Lord Jesus. (Revelation 22:20)

The final prayer of the whole Bible, carrying the earnest longing of the church, the bride, for Christ's return.

## Why It's Worth Memorizing

The verses of Revelation plant the conviction that no matter how great present tribulation appears, sovereignty over history belongs to the Lamb. Memorizing them keeps a believer from despair amid suffering, tunes the ear to the voice of the Lord knocking at the door, and sustains today's faith with hope fixed on his return.
